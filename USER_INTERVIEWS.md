## Interview 1 — P.M.

**Role:** SDE-I  
**Company Stage:** Mid-size engineering/media company

### Conversation Notes

The conversation focused on AI tooling usage inside an engineering team where the tooling was already provisioned by the company.

### Direct Quotes

> "Claude Opus."

> "My company provides it."

> "It works good so I choose it."

> "No."

### Most Surprising Insight

The most surprising insight was how little pricing visibility existed for the end user. The engineer primarily used whichever tool was already available internally, without comparing pricing or evaluating alternatives.

### What It Changed About the Product

This reinforced the idea that many engineers are not directly responsible for AI procurement decisions. Because of that, the audit engine should focus on operational efficiency and organizational visibility rather than assuming users actively compare vendor pricing themselves.

## Interview 2 — S.K.

**Role:** Web Developer  
**Company Stage:** Individual / freelance usage

### Conversation Notes

This conversation represented a very low-spend AI user who primarily relies on free-tier tooling instead of paid subscriptions.

### Direct Quotes

> "I can use only free service of chat gpt tool"

> "some time Microsoft copilots"

> "free service"

### Most Surprising Insight

The strongest insight was that many developers still avoid paid AI subscriptions entirely and switch between free tools opportunistically depending on availability and limits.

### What It Changed About the Product

This directly influenced the “low savings / already optimized” state in the audit results experience. The product should not manufacture fake optimization opportunities for users who already operate on extremely low AI spend.

## Interview 3 — S.P.

**Role:** SDET / QA Engineer  
**Company Stage:** Mid-size engineering team

### Conversation Notes

The conversation focused on perceptions of AI tooling costs and how pricing decisions are evaluated in practice.

### Direct Quotes

> "Claude"

> "Yes a little bit overcharged"

> "Based on the timely and cost per token I guess"

> "I dont have any idea"

### Most Surprising Insight

The most interesting insight was that token pricing awareness existed conceptually, but operational understanding was still unclear in practice. There was a feeling that AI tooling was expensive, but limited visibility into how API/token pricing actually worked.

### What It Changed About the Product

This directly influenced the API optimization logic added to the audit engine. It reinforced the importance of evaluating usage-based pricing alongside retail subscriptions and presenting recommendations in a more understandable way.
